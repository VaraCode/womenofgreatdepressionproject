Short Bio

Dorothea Lange is considered to be one of the most influential American photographers. During The Great Depression, Lange worked for the Farm Security Administration (FSA). Her extensive collection of photographs for the FSA bear witness to the struggles of people trying to survive the greatest economic crisis the country ever experienced.

She was born on May 26, 1895, in Hoboken, New Jersey. As a child, Lange experienced two major traumatic events: she contracted polio and her father abandoned the family. Despite the disease leaving Lange with a limp for the rest of her life, she credited it with shaping the person she became:

"I think it was perhaps the most important thing that happened to me... and formed me, guided me, instructed me, helped me and humiliated me. All those things at once."

Lange added:

"I've never gotten over it, and I am aware of the force and the power of it."

Perhaps her early difficulties gave Lange a keen awareness and empathy for other people's suffering.


In 1960 and 1961, Lange was interviewed by the Regional Cultural Project of UC Berkeley. To listen to her oral history, The Making of A Documentary Photographer visit The Internet Archive.
